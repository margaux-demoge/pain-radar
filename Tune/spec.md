# TUNE

> **The feeling under the feeling, named.** Tune teaches you to read what's happening inside you before it leaks out everywhere else.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

Someone asks how you are and you say fine, and you genuinely cannot tell whether that is true. You can list the things that happened today, you can describe what other people did, you can argue a point, but when it comes to what you actually feel right now, in your body, about your own life, the answer comes back blurry. Then it leaks out the side. You snap at your partner over something small. You cannot sleep and you do not know why. You eat the whole thing in front of the TV and only afterwards realise the day had been bad. You know other people seem to have a clearer signal from themselves, you have read the books, you have done the journalling app for a week and given up. The gap is not knowledge, the gap is the muscle, and the muscle was never built.

**Target users**

Primary: people 22-40 who are intellectually self-aware but emotionally underdeveloped, often high-functioning, often praised for being calm, often blindsided by their own outbursts and slumps. The wedge is the person who has noticed the gap in themselves and is privately tired of being the one who handles things until they cannot.

Not for: people in acute crisis, who need therapy. Tune is a training app for everyday emotional literacy, not a treatment.

## 2. Product experience

Tune is the daily practice that teaches you to read your own signal.

You meet it twice a day, briefly, on your own schedule. In the morning, Tune asks you a short, specific question, not "how are you" but the kind of question a good therapist would have asked, narrow enough to actually answer. You answer in a sentence or in voice. In the evening, it asks one more, this time about something that happened, and helps you name the feeling under the story you are telling about it. The whole thing is two minutes a day, on purpose. You will not commit to ten, and ten would not work better.

The practice itself is the product. Each check-in does a small thing: it teaches you the vocabulary you do not have, the difference between irritated and disrespected, between disappointed and grieving, between tired and depleted. It catches you when you reach for a generic word and asks if a more precise one would land better. Over weeks the vocabulary sticks, and you start using it on yourself in the moment, not just when Tune asks.

Tune also builds a map of you over time. Not a mood graph, a felt map. The themes that keep showing up, the people who keep being involved when you feel a certain way, the gap between what you say you feel and what your behaviour suggests you actually feel. It surfaces this gently and only when it is useful, and it never reduces you to a label. You are not anxious, you are someone who feels untethered in this particular kind of week, and Tune helps you see the pattern next time it shows up.

The app is small on purpose. Two check-ins a day, one short read on Sunday that summarises the week without grading it, and nothing else. No streak punishment, no community, no badges, no premium gating of insights. The bet is that the muscle gets built through tiny consistent reps, not through long heroic sessions, and the product is the schedule that makes the reps happen.

In short: you check in twice a day, and over time your own feelings become legible to you.
