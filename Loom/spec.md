## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You are 40 percent into a cross-stitch piece that will take you another four months. You hold it up to the light and something feels off. Maybe the tension on the dark blue is pulling, maybe the floss you swapped in row 47 doesn't actually match the chart, maybe the whole sky is reading wrong against the cream aida. You scroll through Ravelry, you post in r/CrossStitch, you wait. By the time anyone responds, you have stitched another fifty rows over the thing you should have ripped out, and now ripping it costs a weekend instead of an hour. The piece is solitary by nature, months of needle and floss alone on a sofa, and the only feedback loop you have arrives long after the moment you needed it.

**Target users**

Primary: hobbyist cross-stitchers working on medium-to-large pieces (over 30 hours), past the sampler stage, who have at least one half-finished project in a drawer. They already photograph their WIPs and post them somewhere (Reddit, Instagram, Discord) but get mostly applause and rarely useful critique.

Not for: pattern designers selling to other stitchers (different need), or beginners on first kits who need tutorial content rather than peer review.

## 2. Product experience

Loom is a project log built around one thing: getting another stitcher's eyes on the piece while there is still time to fix it.

Each project lives as its own page from the first stitch. You set it up once: the pattern (linked from a community library or your own upload), the fabric count, the floss brand, your target deadline if you have one. Every time you stitch, you snap a WIP photo, ideally from the same angle, and Loom stacks the photos into a visible progress strip. The strip itself is satisfying enough that stitchers update without being prompted, the way Strava users open the app at the end of a run.

When you want feedback, you tag the WIP with a specific ask: tension on the dark areas, colour reading, pattern accuracy, symmetry, overall finish. The ask is structured, not "thoughts?", so reviewers know what they are looking at. Other stitchers respond with anchored notes: a tap on the spot in the photo where the tension is pulling, a comment naming the floss number that looks off, a flag on a section that does not match the chart. Anchored notes appear pinned to the image, not buried in a comment thread. The target is a useful read within a day, because most stitchers work on a piece every evening and the next session is the moment to act on feedback.

The pattern is a shared object. Every stitcher working from the same chart sees the others' WIPs on the pattern page, with shared notes about confusing sections ("step 12 is unclear, the symbols overlap"). A pattern that has been stitched ten times by Loom users carries collective annotations that make the eleventh stitcher's life easier. Pattern designers can claim their patterns and respond directly. Beginners can browse a pattern's WIP gallery before buying it to see how it actually reads.

Reviewer credibility shows in the small ways that matter to stitchers: number of finished pieces, hours logged, and confirmed-helpful notes (the original stitcher marks a note as useful when they act on it). There is no like count, no follower number, no public leaderboard. The push to finish comes from the project itself: a half-finished project in your roster is visible to your followers as in-progress, and the satisfaction of moving a piece into the Finished shelf is the loop.

In short: every WIP gets eyes on it before the next session, and the projects you start actually get finished.
