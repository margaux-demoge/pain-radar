# EVEN

> **The binge isn't the failure, the restriction is.** Even tracks the loop you're actually in and helps you exit it from the front end, not the back.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You binge, then you hate yourself, then you restrict to make up for it, then a few days in the restriction snaps and you binge again. You've been doing this for years. Every food tracker you've ever used treats the binge as the problem and the diet as the solution, when in fact the diet is the thing that loaded the binge. The apps celebrate streaks of clean eating, which is the exact behavior that's keeping you stuck. You know rationally that the way out is to stop restricting, but every time you try, you end up at the bottom of a bag of something, more ashamed than before. You don't need another calorie counter. You need someone to see the pattern and help you interrupt it without telling you to just have more willpower.

**Target users**

Primary: adults in the restrict-binge cycle who already know they're in one. They've read about intuitive eating, they've tried "just eat normally," and they keep falling back into the loop. They are not in clinical eating disorder treatment.

Not for: people with active anorexia, bulimia, or BED requiring clinical care. Even is not therapy. It is also not for people who want a stricter diet plan.

## 2. Product experience

Even isn't a food tracker. It's a pattern tracker. You log meals as eaten or skipped, and after each one you tap one of three states: ate freely, restricted, or binged. That's the whole input. The app's job is to make the loop visible.

After a few days you start seeing the rhythm of your week as a band of color. Restrictive days stacked together, then a binge, then more restriction, then another binge. For the first time the cycle is not a vague feeling, it's a chart. Even doesn't shame the binge days. It points at the restriction days that came before them and asks if you noticed.

The intervention happens at the front of the loop, not the back. When Even sees you stacking restrictive meals, it interrupts with a small nudge: a permission to eat. Not a meal plan, not a calorie target, just a sentence reminding you that the next meal can be a normal meal, and that doing so is the thing that breaks the cycle.

On binge days Even goes quiet on purpose. There's no make-up plan, no streak break, no penalty. The next morning the app just shows you the loop again, with the binge in its place, and asks what you noticed in the hours before. Over weeks the pattern softens. The bands of restriction shorten, the binges get smaller, the freely-eating green takes more space. Even doesn't promise weight loss. It promises that the cycle gets less violent, and most people who use it find that's the thing they were actually asking for.

In short: Even shows you the restrict-binge loop you're in and helps you exit it by feeding yourself sooner, not harder.
