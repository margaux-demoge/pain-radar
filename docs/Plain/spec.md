## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You wanted to eat better, so you looked it up. Keto says no carbs. Vegan says no animal products. Mediterranean says olive oil. Carnivore says only animal products. Whole30 says no everything. Intermittent fasting says only when. Each tribe is convinced it's right and tells you everyone else is wrong. You can't even buy a yogurt without somebody, somewhere, telling you it's killing you. The result is decision fatigue at every meal, and the safest thing to do is to give up and order pizza, which is the thing none of them wanted. What you actually want is the boring middle, the simple set of rules that anyone with a calm view of nutrition would tell you, without the dogma. No app on the market is willing to give you that, because boring middle doesn't drive subscriptions.

**Target users**

Primary: adults who want to eat better but have been turned off by diet culture. They are not trying to lose major weight, they want energy and confidence around food.

Not for: people with specific medical diets (diabetes, allergies, eating disorders), or people who genuinely thrive in a strict tribe. Plain is for the middle that wants out of the war.

## 2. Product experience

Plain is the world's most boring food app, deliberately. The first thing it does is hand you five rules. Eat protein at every meal. Eat plants at every meal. Drink water. Don't drink calories. Stop when you're full. That's it. Every other piece of advice it gives is downstream of these five.

There is no logging of macros. There is no calorie counter. Instead you tap one of three buttons after each meal: hit the rules, missed one, didn't try. Over a week you see how often you hit, no number, just a small calm chart.

When you're stuck, Plain has a chat that answers food questions in plain English, calibrated to be boring. "Should I eat eggs in the morning?" gets a one-sentence answer that won't make TikTok. The bet is that the absence of drama is the actual product. Nothing in Plain is interesting enough to argue about, which is precisely why it works.

For meals, Plain has a library of fifty recipes, total. Not five hundred. Fifty. Each one is rule-compliant, fast, and uses ingredients you already have. The library deliberately doesn't grow. The premise is that variety is overrated, and that the people who eat best are the people who repeat a small set of meals they like. Plain helps you find your set and rotate it.

The hardest thing Plain does is hold the line against trends. When the latest podcast tells you to fast for sixteen hours, Plain shrugs. When seed oils become the villain of the month, Plain doesn't update. The promise is that what was good for you last year is still good for you this year, and the calm of that is the actual feature.

In short: Plain gives you five simple food rules and refuses to be more interesting than that, so you can stop fighting your food.
