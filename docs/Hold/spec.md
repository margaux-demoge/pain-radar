# HOLD

> **The people you love deserve to be remembered.** Hold makes sure the birthday, anniversary, and follow-up doesn't slip past you again.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You forgot another birthday. Your mom's, your best friend's, your kid's teacher's. You realized at 10pm when Instagram showed you photos. You sent the apology text, again, with the "I'm so sorry I'm the worst" framing, again. The truth is you're not the worst, you're a person with a phone full of dates that are buried in the calendar between work meetings, none of them surfaced when it actually matters. Your phone has every tool to fix this and somehow doesn't. The default reminder is a notification at 9am on the day, which is the worst possible time. The thing you needed was a heads-up three days before, with what to actually do, written by someone who knew your relationship. Forgetting feels small. Repeated, it compounds into a quiet shame about being a bad friend, a bad child, a bad partner.

**Target users**

Primary: adults with a real network of close people they care about, who routinely forget meaningful dates and feel bad about it. Mid-twenties through forties, the friends-with-jobs cohort.

Not for: people who already have a system that works, or people whose social network is small enough not to need help. Hold is for the warm-but-disorganized.

## 2. Product experience

Hold is a single calendar of people, not events. You add the people you actually care about and the dates that matter to them, birthdays, anniversaries, a parent's death anniversary, a friend's annual checkup. The app's job is to keep those dates alive in the right way at the right time.

Three days before each date, Hold pings you. Not on the day, three days before. The notification doesn't say "Sara's birthday is in three days." It says "Sara's birthday is Saturday. Last year you sent her a voice note and she loved it. Want to do that again, or do something else?" The app remembers what you've done in the past and uses it to make the next gesture feel continuous instead of accidental.

For each person Hold builds a small log of moments. What you sent, what they said back, the photo from the dinner. Over years this becomes a quiet record of the relationships you actually have, made of small acts you might otherwise forget. It's not a CRM. It's not networking. It's the opposite, a tool that helps the warm relationships stay warm.

Hold also handles the asymmetric reminders. The friend whose dad died last year, the colleague whose surgery is on Thursday, the mentor who's been having a rough season. You add these once, with a note, and Hold surfaces them at the right window, with a suggested action. The app's whole stance is that being a good friend is mostly remembering, and most people don't remember without help, and that admitting that is the first step out of the shame.

In short: Hold pings you three days before the dates that matter, with what to actually do, so the people you love stop slipping through your calendar.
