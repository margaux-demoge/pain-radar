# ORBIT

> **One person can't be your whole world, even if you want them to be.** Orbit is the gentle pressure that keeps the rest of your life alive when you're falling deep into one person.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You got into the relationship and slowly, without noticing, your friendships went quiet. Your best friend's last text was two weeks ago and you haven't replied. You skipped the group brunch again because your partner wanted to stay in. Your sister called and you said you'd call back and didn't. And the truth is you're fine, you're more than fine, you love this person and you'd rather be with them than anywhere else. But underneath there's a quieter knowledge: you're putting all of yourself into one person, and if anything happened to this relationship you'd have nothing to fall back into. You don't want to be the friend who only resurfaces post-breakup. You also don't have the bandwidth to invent the texts, plan the dinners, and remember the birthdays on top of a full-time job and a partner. The intent is there, the activation energy isn't.

**Target users**

Primary: 25-35 year olds in committed relationships who've noticed their world is shrinking around their partner. Often the more introverted partner, or the partner with a more extroverted significant other whose social calendar has absorbed theirs. They feel quiet guilt about it but don't want to be lectured about "having a life outside your relationship".

Not for: single people building friendships from scratch (different problem, different product), or people with thriving independent social lives already. Orbit is for the slow drift, not the fresh start.

## 2. Product experience

When you set up Orbit, you list the people who matter outside your partner. Not a contact list, a constellation, six to ten names: the best friend you should be texting, the sibling you owe a call, the friend group you used to host dinners with, the coworker who became a real friend. For each, you say roughly how often you want them in your life: weekly, monthly, "I'd just like to see them this year."

Then Orbit does the unglamorous work in the background. It watches the gap, the time since you last actually saw or spoke to each person, against the cadence you set. It doesn't nag. Once a week, it sends you one nudge, the friendship most overdue, with one specific small action: "Anna's gone three weeks. Want to send her a voice note now?" Tap, record, sent. Or "It's been a month since you and the Friday crew did anything, want to put a date on the calendar." The friction is engineered down to seconds.

For the bigger relationships, Orbit helps you plan, not just react. Once a month it asks: who do you want to actually see in person this month, not text. You pick two, Orbit suggests a window in your calendar and drafts the invite. The point is to make the move from "I should see her soon" to "we're seeing each other Thursday" cost you 90 seconds.

What Orbit deliberately doesn't do: rank your relationships, score you against other people, or shame you for the friends you let drift. The tone is "we're keeping your world alive, gently". You can also let people fall off, some friendships are meant to drift, and Orbit asks you to confirm rather than assuming everyone stays on the list forever.

In short: keep the rest of your life alive without it becoming another full-time job.
