## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You and your partner are out of sync about sex. One of you wants more, or wants different, or has stopped wanting at all, and the gap has been there long enough that bringing it up feels like an accusation no matter how you phrase it. So nobody brings it up, and the silence makes everything worse. The version of the conversation that exists in your head is enormous, it is the whole state of the relationship, and you cannot find a way to start it that doesn't feel like dropping a bomb on a Tuesday night. The advice online is either clinical ("schedule sex dates") or oversharing ("communicate openly!") and neither tells you what sentence to actually say first. You don't need couples therapy yet. You need a way to start.

**Target users**

Primary: 26-42 year olds in established relationships (2+ years, often cohabiting or married) where physical intimacy has shifted, slowed, or gone mismatched, and where neither partner is acting on it but both are quietly thinking about it. Both genders, both directions of mismatch. People who would never go to couples therapy as a first move but who can feel the conversation getting heavier the longer it goes unhad.

Not for: new relationships still in the discovery phase, situations involving infidelity or abuse, or solo users looking for sex education content.

## 2. Product experience

Cue is a two-person product. Both partners install it and pair their accounts. From then on, neither of you carries the conversation alone, which is the part that always breaks down. Cue is structured around small cues, single questions or small prompts, one at a time, that one partner can send to the other without it being a confrontation. "What's a moment recently you felt close to me physically." "Is there something you've been curious about and haven't said." "What would make you feel wanted this week." Each cue is short, easy to answer, and lives inside the app, not in your text thread that you both reread anxiously.

The receiving partner has time. There is no read-receipt pressure, no notification urgency. You answer when you have the words. The other partner sees your answer, you see theirs, and that is one cue done. No essay, no resolution, just one piece of the conversation that you would otherwise never have started. Cues come from a library curated with sex therapists, not as a quiz, as a scaffold. You can request a cue, your partner can send one, and Cue suggests what is appropriate for where you are in the conversation.

Over time, the cues build a shape. After a dozen, you have a real picture of where your partner is and where you are, not by talking about the whole thing at once, but by accumulating small honesties that would have been impossible to say in one sitting. When you do finally have the bigger conversation, you both walk in with context, and the conversation is twenty times shorter.

Cue stays out of the bedroom itself. It does not schedule, it does not gamify desire, it does not turn intimacy into a tracked metric. It is only the conversation, the part that was missing, kept small and survivable until it is no longer the thing you are avoiding.

In short: the hard conversation about sex, broken into pieces small enough that you can actually start.
