> **The family you didn't choose, navigated.** Inlaw maps the politics, drafts the lines, and tracks the small wins that turn five years of tension into a stable peace.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You love your partner. You did not pick their family. And their family, depending on the week, is the mother who finds something wrong with the way you load the dishwasher, the ex-wife who texts at 11pm, the brother who hasn't spoken to your partner in two years, or the parents who still haven't acknowledged you exist after five years together. The problem isn't the family, it's that the cost of dealing with them falls on you twice: once when you absorb the slight, and again when you try to talk to your partner about it and they get defensive about their own people. You can't say "your mother was rude" without it becoming a fight. You can't say nothing without resenting them both. There's no playbook for this, and the generic advice ("set boundaries", "communicate") is uselessly abstract when you're standing in their kitchen on Christmas Eve.

**Target users**

Primary: 26-40 year olds in serious partnerships where the in-law dynamic is a recurring drain, often the partner who married into a tighter or more culturally specific family than their own. Often female (the gendered labour of family-managing is real), but not exclusively. They've had the same five conversations with friends and are looking for something more structured than venting.

Not for: family-of-origin issues (Inlaw is about the family you married into, not yours), or for couples where the partner-relationship itself is the problem. Inlaw assumes the partnership is solid and the family is the friction.

## 2. Product experience

You start by mapping the cast. Who's who in this family: names, roles, the dynamics that matter (mum critical of you, brother estranged, ex-wife co-parenting). You can be specific or rough, Inlaw learns as you go. This isn't a CRM, it's a sketch of the system you're navigating.

When something happens, you log it, three sentences, the moment, what was said, how it landed. Over time Inlaw shows you the pattern you couldn't see in the heat of it: it's not random, it's always the same person, always around the same trigger (a holiday, a money topic, the kids). Seeing the pattern is half the relief, because suddenly the thing isn't your fault, it's the system's behaviour.

The other half is the line. Before the next dinner, the next call, the next visit, you can ask Inlaw for a line: something to say if the topic comes up, something to say if the boundary gets pushed, something to say to your partner afterward. The lines aren't scripts, they're calibrated to the specific person and the specific trigger Inlaw has watched you log. "If she comments on your job again, here's a way to deflect that doesn't escalate and doesn't fold." You can rehearse it or just keep it in your back pocket.

Over months, Inlaw tracks the small wins, the visits that didn't blow up, the comments you didn't take the bait on, the moments your partner backed you up. Most people in long in-law conflicts forget they've had any wins at all. Seeing them lined up changes the felt narrative from "this never gets better" to "we're slowly learning to handle this", which is usually the truth.

In short: stop walking into the same dinner cold. See the system, prepare the line, and remember the wins.
