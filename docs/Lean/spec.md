# LEAN

> **You know you push people away. You don't know how to stop in the moment.** Lean catches the urge to bolt and asks one question that lets you stay.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You've done the work. You've read about attachment styles. You know you're avoidant. You can describe, in detail, the pattern: you meet someone, things get close, you start to feel claustrophobic, you pick a fight or go cold or ghost, and a week later you're alone again wondering why. The diagnosis isn't the problem. The problem is that in the exact moment the urge to pull away hits, the urge feels like truth. They're too much. They want too much. You need space. It's only a week later, when you're alone, that you see it was the pattern again, not them. You don't need another article. You need something that intervenes in the eight seconds between "I should reply to this text" and "I'm not going to reply to this text", because that's where the whole life-pattern gets written.

**Target users**

Primary: 22-35 year olds who self-identify as avoidant or fearful-avoidant, who've done the reading and are committed to changing the pattern but keep losing in the moment. Skews slightly male but increasingly mixed as attachment-style content has gone mainstream. They want a co-conspirator, not a therapist.

Not for: people in genuinely unsafe relationships where pulling away is the right move, or people who haven't yet recognised the pattern as theirs. Lean assumes the diagnostic is settled and the work is execution.

## 2. Product experience

You set Lean up with a few of your patterns: the situations that tend to trigger your retreat (someone gets emotional, things get serious, they want to define the relationship, they ask for something), and the moves you tend to make (ghost, pick a fight, withdraw affection, go cold for a week).

Then you carry it with you. When you feel the urge starting, you open Lean and tag it: "she just sent a long emotional text and I want to disappear." Lean doesn't tell you what to do. It asks one question, drawn from your own patterns: "Last time you felt this, what did you do? What happened next?" Or "Is the urge in proportion to what just happened, or does it feel bigger than the trigger?" The questions are designed to slow the eight seconds into eighty, just long enough for your prefrontal cortex to come back online.

Then Lean offers one small move, the smallest possible counter-move that isn't ghosting. Not "have a vulnerable conversation", not "process your feelings". Just: "send a holding message, even one sentence. 'I read this, I need a few hours, I'll come back.' That's enough." The bar is engineered for the avoidant brain: not heroic, just present.

Over time, Lean shows you the wins. The texts you almost didn't send and did. The conversations you almost ducked and stayed for. The relationships that didn't end this time. Avoidant people don't usually see their wins because the wins are invisible (the thing that didn't go wrong). Lean keeps the receipts so the pattern shift becomes legible to you.

In short: catch the urge to bolt, slow it down enough to choose, and bank the small wins that are the actual change.
