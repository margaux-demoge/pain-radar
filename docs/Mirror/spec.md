## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You caught your reflection in a window and lost the next hour to it. Or you saw a photo of yourself someone posted and now you cannot look at it. Or you are in bed running a slideshow of the parts of your face and your body that are wrong, comparing them to people who are not real, scrolling for evidence that confirms the worst version. You know rationally that nobody is staring at the thing you cannot stop staring at, you know the spiral is bigger than the cause, you know the right thing to do is close the camera and put the phone down. You cannot do it. The loop closes around you and the rest of the day flattens. Therapy helps if you can afford it and find it. Self-help books help if you can read them on a calm day. The thing you actually need is something that meets you inside the spiral, at 11pm with the phone in your hand.

**Target users**

Primary: people 16-35 with sub-clinical but persistent appearance anxiety, the loops that do not meet the bar for body dysmorphic disorder but still steal hours, days, and confidence. The wedge is the late-night spiraller: the person whose worst moments happen alone with a mirror or a screen.

Not for: people with diagnosed BDD or eating disorders who need clinical care. Mirror is a self-soothing tool, not a treatment, and it is built to refer out when the loop is bigger than what it can hold.

## 2. Product experience

Mirror is the friend who picks up the phone when the spiral starts.

You open it when you are already in it. The first screen does not ask you to log a mood or rate your anxiety on a scale, it asks you what just happened. The photo you saw, the mirror you passed, the comment someone made, the thing you noticed about your own face. You type or talk, and Mirror reads the spiral, not the trigger. It names what is happening in plain language: this is a comparison loop, this is the catastrophising part, this is the part where you are searching for evidence to confirm what you already decided.

Then it gets you out. Not with a breathing exercise from a generic library, with something that fits the specific shape of the loop you are in. Sometimes that is a grounding technique. Sometimes it is a reframe you would not have reached on your own. Sometimes it is a hard fact about the math of how often other people are actually noticing what you are noticing. Sometimes it is just sitting with you for five minutes while the wave passes, which is often all the spiral needed in the first place. The interaction is short, the tone is steady, and the goal is one thing: get you to a place where you can put the phone down and go to bed, or back to work, or back to your friends.

Over time Mirror learns your loops. The triggers that get you the most, the times of day they hit, the spirals you keep coming back to. It surfaces patterns gently, without making the patterns themselves into another thing to spiral about. You start to recognise the loop before it closes, and the recognition itself starts to shorten it.

Mirror refuses to be a place where appearance anxiety is content. There are no before/afters, no glow-up timelines, no community feed of other people's flaws. There is no streak to maintain that would punish you for not having a bad day. It is closed, private, and small on purpose. The bet is that the spiral does not need an audience, it needs an exit.

In short: when the spiral starts, you open Mirror, and Mirror gets you out.
