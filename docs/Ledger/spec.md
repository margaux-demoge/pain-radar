# LEDGER

> **A private record of who's been carrying the relationship.** Not to keep score, to stop pretending the scoring isn't already happening in your head.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You are the one who texts first, plans the dates, books the table, remembers the birthday, asks how the meeting went. Your partner is lovely, and you love them, and you also cannot ignore the small voice that has started saying you are doing all of this on your own. You do not want to bring it up because the moment you do, it sounds petty, you sound like you are keeping score, and the conversation collapses into "well I do other things". But you are keeping score, quietly, all the time, because the imbalance is real and you have nowhere to put it. The choice today is either stay silent and let resentment build, or raise it and be told you are being needy. There is no third option that lets you see the pattern clearly before you decide whether it is worth a conversation.

**Target users**

Primary: 25-40 year olds in committed relationships (dating seriously or cohabiting) who feel like the over-functioning partner and have started to wonder if it's a story they're telling themselves or a real pattern. Mostly women, but not only. They are competent, self-aware, allergic to therapy-speak, and would rather have data than feelings to bring to the conversation.

Not for: people in genuinely abusive or one-sided dynamics where the answer is to leave, not to log. Ledger assumes a relationship worth examining, not one worth escaping.

## 2. Product experience

Ledger is a private journal, not a couples app. Only you use it. Your partner never sees it, never knows it exists unless you tell them. The first time you open it, Ledger asks what kinds of effort matter to you in this relationship: initiating contact, planning shared time, emotional check-ins, logistics, repair after a fight. You pick what's load-bearing for you. Everything else stays off the page.

Day to day, Ledger is light. When you initiate something, you tap a small log. When your partner does, you tap the other side. It takes two seconds. No notifications nagging you, no streaks, no gamification. The log is just a quiet shelf you put things on. Most days you forget about it, and that is fine, the patterns that matter show up over weeks, not hours.

After two weeks Ledger shows you what's actually there. Not a scorecard with a number, a picture: over the last fourteen days, you initiated 9 out of 11 plans, you sent the first text 12 mornings out of 14, your partner reached out unprompted twice. The point is not to win the argument, the point is to see whether the voice in your head is matching reality. Sometimes it isn't, and Ledger lets you put the resentment down. Sometimes it is, and now you have something concrete instead of a vibe.

When you decide it's time to talk, Ledger helps you turn the picture into one sentence. Not "you never plan anything", which is an accusation, but "I noticed I've been the one starting most of our weeks lately, can we look at that together". You can show your partner the picture if you want, or keep it to yourself and just use it to know what you actually feel. The product never tells you what to do with what you see.

In short: a small private log that turns the score you're already keeping into something you can finally look at honestly.
