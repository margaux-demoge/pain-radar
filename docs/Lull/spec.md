# LULL

> **A bedtime routine that adapts to your kid, not the other way around.** Lull learns what works in your house and stops you from white-knuckling the same broken routine every night.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

It's 7:30pm, you've been parenting for twelve hours, and the only thing standing between you and a quiet evening is bedtime. Which means another forty-five minutes of negotiating, re-reading, fetching water, putting them back in bed, until either they fall asleep or you do, and the rest of your evening is gone. You've read the sleep books. You've tried the routines. None of them survive contact with your actual kid. The advice online assumes a generic toddler and a generic parent, and yours are neither. By the time bedtime is over you're too fried to be a partner, a person, or anything other than a body on the couch. You don't need a sleep training program. You need someone to tell you what to try next, given your kid, your week, and your patience level tonight.

**Target users**

Primary: parents of one to six year olds who have bedtime drama nearly every night and feel their evenings are being eaten alive. They've tried at least one sleep-training book or app and given up.

Not for: parents of infants in the cry-it-out window (this is not sleep training), or families with a clinical sleep issue requiring medical attention.

## 2. Product experience

Lull starts with a five-minute interview about your kid, your routine, and what's currently breaking. It doesn't hand you a template. It builds a routine based on what you already do, and tells you which two or three things to change. Maybe it's the order of bath and book. Maybe it's the lighting. Maybe it's that your partner needs to be the one to do the last handoff. The first prescription is small enough that you'll actually try it tonight.

Each evening Lull pings you at the start of your routine and walks you through the steps, in order, on the lock screen. After bedtime closes, it asks one question: how did tonight go, on a 1 to 5. That's the whole logging burden. Over two weeks the app starts to see what's working and what isn't. If Mondays are always rough because of swim class, it notices. If the routine cracks when your partner is traveling, it notices.

When something stops working Lull doesn't tell you to be more consistent. It tells you what to try instead. It has a library of moves, longer wind-down, dropped nap, earlier dinner, a transition object, and it suggests one based on what it's seen in your data. You try it for three nights, log how it went, and the app either commits the change or rolls it back.

The thing Lull deliberately doesn't do is shame. There is no streak, no perfect-routine score, no comparison to other families. The only metric the app cares about is how many minutes bedtime took, trending down. When you log a bad night, the app's response is to suggest a smaller routine for tomorrow, not a harder one.

In short: Lull is the bedtime coach that watches your nights and tells you one thing to change next, not twenty.
