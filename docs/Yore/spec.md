## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You scroll past a post about the smell of a Blockbuster on a Friday night, or the specific way the school bell sounded, or the song that played at every middle school dance, and your whole chest does a small thing. For two seconds you remember being eleven. Then the next post is a recipe and the moment is gone. The internet is full of these flashes, but they are scattered across Reddit threads, Facebook groups your aunt is in, TikToks you cannot find again, Twitter accounts that get suspended. There is no one place where the artefacts of your specific generation live, no place to add yours when one comes to you in the shower, no place to read them in a long quiet stretch the way you used to read magazines. The shared cultural memory of an entire age cohort, the millennials, the Gen Z, the cusps, is real and active and has no home.

**Target users**

Primary: people 25-40 who grew up online but not entirely online, and whose nostalgia for their childhood and adolescence is unusually vivid because the artefacts of it (chat clients, ringtones, ad jingles, specific brands) disappeared overnight. The wedge is the rememberer: the person who already drops "remember when…" into group chats and is met with instant recognition.

Not for: people looking for current-events social feeds, or anyone whose feed of choice is more about today than yesterday. Yore is deliberately backward-facing.

## 2. Product experience

Yore is a feed made entirely of the things your generation forgot it remembered.

When you open it, you do not see today's posts, you see memories. Short, specific, generational: the loading sound of dial-up, the exact font of mid-2000s MSN, the way a teacher would say a word, the candy that only existed for two years. Each one is a small card, sometimes a photo, sometimes a few sentences, sometimes a short video. You scroll, and every couple of cards something hits and you stop, and you say out loud "oh my god, yes" to no one. That is the entire emotional contract of the app.

You add yours when one surfaces. The format is forgiving. A sentence and a tag is enough. A photo with no caption is enough. You do not need to be a creator, you just need to be old enough to remember. Yore handles the categorising, the tagging by year and theme, and the matching to other people who lit up at the same thing. Posts are surfaced not by recency but by resonance: things that pulled a reaction out of a lot of people in your cohort rise, things that did not, sink quietly.

Yore is structured around your cohort, not just your account. When you sign up you tell it the years you went through school, and from then on the feed leans into your specific window. A 1989 baby and a 2002 baby do not see the same Yore, and they are not supposed to. You can browse other cohorts when you are curious about what your older sibling or your younger cousin grew up with, but your home feed is the one that hits.

The comments are short on purpose. Yore is not Twitter and not Reddit, it is a memory feed, and the value is in the shared "I remember that too" not in the take. You can react with a small set of cohort-coded responses, and you can reply, but the design pushes the energy back into adding your own memory rather than dragging out the discussion.

In short: you open Yore, you scroll, and your past surfaces.
