## 1. Overview

**Problem**

Another kid at the playground is doing something yours isn't. Walking, talking, sharing, reading, whatever. You go home and Google it, and within four clicks you're reading about early intervention, autism markers, gifted programs, and a forum thread from 2014 that's still keeping you up at night. The internet has no calibrated voice on child development. Every result is either a panic generator or a vague reassurance, and neither tells you what you actually want to know: is my kid in the normal range for their age, and at what point should I actually do something. Pediatricians say "they'll catch up" without showing you the data. The internet says "they might never catch up" without showing you the data. Nobody just shows you the data.

**Target users**

Primary: parents of kids two to seven who compare their kid to other kids and have at least one specific area of low-grade worry. They are healthy, anxious googlers, not parents in active concern with a clinical team.

Not for: parents of kids with diagnosed delays already under clinical care, or parents who genuinely never worry about milestones. Range is for the worried-but-not-yet-acting middle.

---

## 2. Product experience

Range starts by asking you to pick one or two specific things you're worried about. Speech, motor, social, attention, reading. For each, it shows you the actual normal range from real developmental data, not a single average. You see that walking has a normal window of nine to eighteen months, that first sentences cover a band of eighteen to thirty months, and that your kid sits well inside that band even though the kid down the street started six months earlier.

For each area Range gives you three things: the normal range, the actual threshold where most pediatricians would refer for evaluation, and the small everyday things you can do that have evidence behind them. Not "read to your kid more," but specifically what kind of interaction at what age moves the needle. It's the conversation you wish you could have with a pediatrician for an hour, distilled into a calm screen.

If your kid is inside the range, the app tells you so, in plain language, and asks if you want to check back in a month. If your kid is approaching the threshold, the app tells you so, calmly, and walks you through what to ask your pediatrician at the next visit. There is no diagnosis, no scoring, no scary chart. Range is explicit about what it can and cannot say.

The deeper value is what Range doesn't do. It doesn't send you to a forum. It doesn't show you ads for early intervention services. It doesn't keep you scrolling. Once it has answered your question for the week, it closes the conversation, because the goal is for you to stop worrying, not to come back daily.

In short: Range shows you where your kid actually sits in the normal range and tells you, honestly, when it's worth doing something about it.
