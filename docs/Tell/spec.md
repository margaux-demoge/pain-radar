## 1. Overview

**Problem**

Someone is in your head. They texted back fast yesterday, then took a day. They lean in when you talk, but they also do that with everyone. A friend says "they're obviously into you", another says "don't get your hopes up", and now you are screenshotting messages to a group chat, reading replies six times, replaying the dinner in your head trying to decide whether the way they touched your arm meant something. You are not asking for a relationship coach or a therapist, you just want one calm voice that looks at the same evidence you have and tells you, honestly, what it adds up to. Right now that voice lives inside group chats, Reddit threads, and 2am scrolls through dating advice videos, and none of them know your situation. The uncertainty itself is exhausting, and the cost of misreading it goes in both directions.

**Target users**

Primary: people 18-30 in the early ambiguous stage of a romantic interest, friends-to-something, coworker crush, three dates in and unsure. They already spend hours debriefing with friends and consuming relationship content, and they are looking for a more focused, less judgmental read. The wedge is the over-analyzer: the person who notices everything, remembers every text, and is exhausted by their own loop.

Not for: people in established relationships looking for couples advice, or anyone hoping the app will tell them what to do next. Tell reads signals, it does not prescribe moves.

## 2. Product experience

Tell is a calm second opinion on the situation you cannot stop thinking about.

You open the app and there is no profile, no quiz, no onboarding to power through. You just start telling it about them. Who they are to you, how long this has been going, and then the things that actually live in your head: the screenshot of the message you cannot read, the moment at the bar, the way they reacted when you brought up that thing. You can paste, type, or talk. Tell builds a picture of the situation from what you give it, asks a few targeted follow-ups about the parts that matter, and never asks for more than it needs.

Then it reads the situation back to you. Not as a verdict and not as a horoscope, as a structured read: here is what their behavior is consistent with, here is what it is not consistent with, here is the part that is genuinely ambiguous and why. The tone is the friend who has known you for ten years and is not afraid to say the uncomfortable thing, but who also refuses to invent drama where there is none. When the signals are mixed, Tell says so. When they are clear in either direction, it says that too.

You can come back the next day and add what happened next. The new text. The way they acted when you saw them again. Tell holds the thread across days and weeks, and the read gets sharper as more real evidence accumulates. Nothing is shared, nothing is posted, no one else sees it. It is your situation, in one place, with one steady voice attached to it.

Tell deliberately stops short of telling you what to do. It will not write your next message, it will not script your next conversation, it will not push you toward a confession. The bet is that the loop costing you sleep is not "what should I say", it is "what is actually going on", and once that part lands, the next move becomes obvious on its own.

In short: you describe what they did, Tell tells you what it means.
