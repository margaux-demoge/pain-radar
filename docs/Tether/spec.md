# TETHER

> **Five minutes of you, fully there, counts.** Tether helps you stitch together the small presence moments that actually build a kid who feels held.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You love your kid. And at the end of most days you can't remember the last time you sat down and were actually with them, phone away, no other agenda, just them. You meant to. You always mean to. Then work happens, dinner happens, the laundry happens, and the day ends with a goodnight kiss and a quiet certainty that you didn't show up the way you wanted to. It builds. After weeks of this you start to feel like a stranger in your own house, like your kid is being raised by your phone and the schedule and the routine, and you're just the logistics manager. None of the parenting apps speak to this. They track milestones, screen time, behavior. None of them track the thing you're actually grieving, which is your own attention.

**Target users**

Primary: working parents of kids aged two to ten who feel chronically distracted and guilty about not being present. They have the resources to parent well, they just can't find the moments.

Not for: parents seeking a screen-time-for-kids app, or parents with abundant time who don't experience this guilt. Tether is for the time-starved, attention-starved parent.

## 2. Product experience

Tether is the smallest thing that could possibly work. Once a day, at a moment you choose, it pings you with a single suggestion: a five-minute presence prompt sized to your kid's age. Sometimes it's a question to ask them at dinner. Sometimes it's a game that fits in a car ride. Sometimes it's just "put your phone in the other room for ten minutes." The prompts are written to be doable, not aspirational.

When you do one, you tap a button to say so. That's the whole logging surface. Over a week the app builds a quiet record of the moments you showed up: Tuesday breakfast question, Thursday bath time, Saturday morning walk. It's not a streak, not a score. It's a stitched-together view of presence, the antidote to the feeling that none of it is happening.

If you miss days, Tether doesn't punish. It softens. The next prompt comes a little easier, a little shorter, sometimes literally "look at them across the room and smile, that counts." The app's posture is that presence is built in micro-doses, and that fifteen of those across a week add up to a real relationship.

On Sunday evening Tether shows you the week as a small mosaic of moments and asks you to write one thing your kid did that you want to remember. Over months this becomes a private record of who your kid is becoming, kept by the only person who can keep it. Tether doesn't make you a better parent. It makes the version of you that wants to be there visible to yourself, on a daily basis, until that version is the default.

In short: Tether logs the small moments of presence you actually have, so you stop feeling like a stranger in your own house.
