# CRADLE

> **A health plan that respects the fact you just had a baby.** Cradle is the postpartum coach that lowers the bar on purpose, so you actually clear it.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You had a baby, and the version of healthy that worked before isn't possible now. You can't meal-prep on Sundays, you can't carve out an hour for a workout, you can't sleep enough to recover from anything. Every app you open still thinks you're the person you were before. They show you streaks you'll never keep, calorie targets that assume a calm kitchen, workout plans that need uninterrupted time. So you fail them within a week, feel worse about yourself, and quit. The guilt is the worst part: you know you should be taking care of your body to take care of your kid, and instead you feel like you're failing both. There is no plan that starts from the assumption you have twenty minutes a day, broken into three-minute pieces, and a body that's still healing.

**Target users**

Primary: parents in the first eighteen months postpartum who used to track their health pre-baby and have given up because nothing fits anymore. Mostly mothers, but the new dad in survival mode counts too.

Not for: people pre-pregnancy, people who already have stable postpartum routines, or people looking for clinical postpartum care. Cradle is for the in-between: not sick, but not okay either.

## 2. Product experience

Cradle starts by asking how old the baby is, how the birth went, and how much support you actually have. From those three answers it builds a baseline that's deliberately lower than anything else you've ever used. The daily ask isn't a workout or a meal plan, it's three things, sized to your week: one piece of movement that fits in the time you actually have, one food intention that doesn't require cooking, and one moment of rest.

Each day Cradle opens with a check-in that takes ten seconds. How did you sleep, on a 1 to 5, and is the baby in a hard week. If the baby is in a hard week, Cradle drops the bar further on its own. No streak breaks, no guilt copy, no make-up sessions. The app explicitly tells you that doing less this week is the right answer.

When you do manage something, Cradle counts it generously. A walk to the mailbox with the stroller is logged as movement. A protein bar eaten standing up is logged as a meal. Five minutes of sitting alone in the car is logged as rest. The point is to make the lowest baseline visible, so you can see you are in fact doing things, even on the days that feel like nothing.

Every two weeks Cradle pulls back and shows you what changed. Not a weight number, not a body composition chart, but how often you moved, how your sleep score is trending, how your mood is reading. If something is sliding, it nudges one small lever, never five. Cradle is not a postpartum fitness app. It is a permission slip with a memory.

In short: Cradle lowers the bar to where you actually are, then helps you nudge it up one quiet inch at a time.
