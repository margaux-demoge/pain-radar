# SPAN

> **The shape of the time between visits.** Span turns the gap of being apart into something you're building together, not just enduring.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You are six weeks from seeing them again, and the days in between have started to blur. The good-morning text, the dinner photo, the call before bed, all the rituals that were supposed to hold you together are starting to feel like obligations, and you can both feel it. When you actually talk, half the call is logistics and the other half is each of you reassuring the other that you are still okay. The relationship is fine, but the time apart has no shape, it is just a hole you are waiting to climb out of. Couples who survive distance are the ones who build something inside the gap instead of just waiting for it to end, and nobody teaches you how to do that. The apps that exist are either glorified countdown timers or shared calendars dressed up with hearts, neither helps you actually feel close on a Tuesday at 9pm when there is nothing to say.

**Target users**

Primary: 20-32 year olds in long-distance relationships of at least three months who have already survived the honeymoon and hit the grind of the middle stretch. Often students, early-career, or temporarily separated for work or family reasons. They are committed enough to be in it, and frustrated enough to look for help.

Not for: situationships, couples in the first weeks of distance (still on adrenaline), or relationships where the distance is the symptom of something already broken.

## 2. Product experience

Span starts with the visit. The two of you set the next time you'll be in the same room, even if it's vague ("end of August, probably the 20th"), and Span lays out the shape of the time until then. Not a sad countdown, a span, a stretch of days with texture. Inside that stretch, Span suggests a small handful of moments to do together across the distance: not nightly check-ins, three or four real things spread across the gap. A shared movie night with synced playback. A questions-game you each fill in at your own pace and reveal together. A photo trade of "what your Tuesday actually looks like" so the other person can picture the room.

The point of these moments is not the activity, it's that they leave a trail. By the time the visit arrives, you've made fifteen or twenty small things together that you can both point to. The middle of the distance stops being a void and starts being a record.

Span also holds the boring stuff that distance couples actually fight about. Whose turn it is to schedule the next call. What time zone you're in this week. When you'll next have a free evening. Not a shared calendar with everything in it, just the few logistics that, left implicit, become the source of the "I feel like I'm doing all the work" fight that every distance couple has at month four.

When the visit finally happens, Span goes quiet. It is not the point of the relationship, the point is the time together. Span keeps the record of what you built across the last stretch, and when the next distance starts, you open it back up.

In short: distance with a shape, so the time apart becomes something you made together, not just got through.
