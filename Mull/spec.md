# MULL

> **A place to think honestly about whether this is the right person.** Not a decision tool, a thinking tool, for the question you can't ask anyone you know.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

The relationship is good. They are kind, you are happy most weeks, your friends like them, and still, in quiet moments, you keep asking yourself if this is the one. Not because something is wrong, because something feels uncertain in a way you cannot name. You cannot ask your friends without sounding like you are about to break up. You cannot ask your therapist every week. You cannot ask your partner without it becoming the question itself. So you carry it alone, and it gets louder over months, and eventually the not-knowing becomes its own pressure, separate from whatever was originally there. The actual question, is this cold feet or is this real incompatibility, has no honest place to live. Journaling helps some people but most of us do not journal, and even when we do, we tend to write the version we already agree with.

**Target users**

Primary: 24-35 year olds in relationships of 1-4 years that are approaching a decision point, moving in, getting engaged, having kids, or simply hitting the moment where the question stops being theoretical. Not unhappy, not in crisis, but unclear. Often analytical by temperament, the people who would build a spreadsheet if they thought it would help, and who know a spreadsheet won't.

Not for: people already mostly decided who want validation, or people in active distress who need a therapist, not an app.

## 2. Product experience

Mull does not ask you to rate your partner. The premise of every "compatibility quiz" is that incompatibility is what you are looking for, and that is rarely the actual question. Mull asks you to notice. Each week it offers one small prompt, not about your relationship in the abstract, about a specific moment from the week just past. "Think of a time this week you felt fully at ease with them. What were you doing." Or "think of a moment this week you held something back. What was it." Or "when did you feel most yourself around them this week." You write a few sentences, or you skip. Nothing is required.

Over weeks, Mull shows you what you've actually been writing, not as a verdict, as a mirror. The recurring textures, the things you keep flagging, the moments you keep returning to. Without pushing an interpretation. If every week the moment you held something back was the same kind of moment, you see that pattern yourself. If the moments of ease keep showing up and outnumber the rest, you see that too. Mull never says "you should leave" or "you should stay". It is not qualified to, and pretending it is would be the worst thing it could do.

Mull also gives you somewhere to put the spiraling thoughts. The 2am "what if I'm wrong" loop, the comparison to a friend's relationship, the doubt that surges and recedes. You write it, you mark it as a spiral, and Mull tucks it away. Two weeks later it shows you what you wrote, and most of the time the heat is gone and you can read it honestly. Some spirals turn out to be signal. Most are just spirals. Sorting one from the other is what Mull is for.

What you write is yours. No cloud sync if you don't want it, no sharing, no AI summary that "knows you better than you know yourself". Mull is a quiet room with a window, not a coach.

In short: a place to think about the question without having to ask it yet.
