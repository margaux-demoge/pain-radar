## 1. Overview

**Problem**

You bake alone. The loaf comes out of the dutch oven, you wait an hour, you cut into it, and there it is, the crumb you have been trying to fix for three weeks. Too tight. Too gummy. Big holes only at the top. You stand in your kitchen with a slice in your hand and no idea what it means. Was the starter underfed? Did you overproof the bulk? Was the hydration too low for this flour? You scroll through Reddit comments on someone else's loaf hoping yours looks close, you compare crumb shots on Instagram, you read forum threads from 2019. By the time you decide what to try next, four days have passed and the question has gone cold. You wanted a hobby, not a science fair, and you keep baking the same near-miss loaf because there is no one in the room to look at it with you.

**Target users**

Primary: home bakers who have made between 5 and 50 sourdough loaves, are past the "is my starter alive" phase, and are now actively trying to read their own crumb to improve. They already lurk on r/Sourdough and have posted a crumb shot at least once. They bake at least every two weeks and care about getting better, not just feeding the family.

Not for: pure beginners who do not yet have a starter or a routine, and not for professional bakers who already work with mentors and don't need a community read.

## 2. Product experience

Crumb is one thing: a place where every loaf you cut gets read by people who know what they are looking at, within the hour.

It starts with the slice. You cut the loaf, you take a clean photo of the crumb against a neutral surface, and you open Crumb. The post is structured, not free-form: hydration, flour mix, bulk time and temperature, cold retard length, shape, dutch oven or open. You tap through it in under a minute because the fields are presets, not essays. You hit post, and your loaf lands in the Fresh queue where other bakers are scrolling in real time, the way r/Sourdough's new feed works but built around diagnosis instead of applause.

The feedback comes back as structured reads, not comments. A reader taps your post and chooses one of a small set of diagnoses: underproofed, overproofed, weak starter, hydration too low, hydration too high, poor shaping, oven too cool, looks great. They can add one short note. You see the diagnoses stack: four people say overproofed, one says weak starter, two say it looks fine. The signal is in the cluster, not in any single comment. The target is the first read within fifteen minutes and a confident cluster within the hour, because you are still in your kitchen and the next bake is tomorrow.

The readers are not random. Every baker on Crumb has a history: their own loaves, the diagnoses they have given, the ones that were later confirmed by the original baker. Accuracy compounds into a reader score, and high-score readers float to the top of every post. A beginner can still chime in, but their read is weighted lighter. There is no karma chase, no rank to grind, just a quiet reputation that says this person sees crumb clearly. The leaderboard exists but it is local to the diagnosis surface, not a public feed.

Your own loaves stack into a timeline. Each bake sits next to the last one with the variables you changed pinned to it, so the third time you bump hydration from 72 to 75 you can see what shifted in the crumb. The community feedback rides along, so the pattern is not just in your photos, it is in what other people saw. Over ten loaves, a baker starts to read their own crumb without needing to ask.

In short: you post the crumb, the community diagnoses it within the hour, and over a few loaves you stop guessing.
