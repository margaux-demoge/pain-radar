# MEND

> **The bridge back when you've gone quiet on each other.** Mend gives you the first sentence after the silence, so you don't have to invent it from scratch.

## 1. Overview

**Problem**

The fight wasn't even a big one, but you both went silent and now it's been three days. You're sleeping in the same bed and texting "ok" about the groceries and neither of you wants to be the one to break first. You know what to say if you could just open your mouth, but every opening sounds wrong: too dramatic ("we need to talk"), too small ("hi"), too defensive ("I've been thinking and"). So you wait, and the longer you wait the bigger the silence gets, until reconciling feels like more work than just letting it become the new normal. Drift doesn't happen in one moment, it happens in a hundred unrepaired three-day silences. You've watched friends' relationships die this way. You don't want yours to.

**Target users**

Primary: 22-38 year olds in established relationships (cohabiting or close to it) who've noticed the post-fight silences getting longer or the post-trip distances getting harder to close. They're not in crisis, they're competent at most things, but they freeze at the "first sentence after". They want a small, dignified prompt, not a couples retreat.

Not for: pre-conflict couples (Mend assumes there's something to repair), or couples where one partner is unwilling, the product needs both sides to lean in even slightly.

## 2. Product experience

Mend opens with one question: what happened. You write a few sentences, your version, raw. Mend asks two follow-ups, never more: what do you want from the next conversation, and what are you afraid will happen if you start it. Three minutes of writing, max.

Then Mend gives you a single message, drafted for you, in a tone calibrated to your answers. Not a scripted apology, not a "I-statement" template, a real opening sentence that fits the specific texture of what happened, in your register. If you said the fight was about household stuff but the real thing is you feel unseen, Mend opens with the real thing, gently. If you said you mostly want to laugh it off, Mend opens light. You can rewrite, regenerate, or send. The act of seeing one good first sentence is usually enough to unlock the next one yourself.

After the conversation, Mend asks how it went. Not for analytics, for you. Couples who reconnect often don't remember what they actually said, so the slow drift goes unrepaired by accident. Mend keeps a quiet log: what you fought about, what you said to come back, what worked. Six months in, you can look back and see "we've come back from this kind of silence before, three times. We will again." It's a small thing, but it changes how the next silence feels.

For longer drifts (a partner came back from a long trip, you've been roommates for a month), Mend offers a different kind of opening: not a message, a 20-minute structured conversation prompt for the two of you to do in the same room. Three questions, taking turns, no interruptions. The mechanic is borrowed from couples-therapy practice but stripped of its clinical weight.

In short: when the silence has gone on too long, Mend gives you the sentence that breaks it.
